Context

Why real estate teams needed a better CRM

Real estate sales is fundamentally different from SaaS or e-commerce. A deal takes weeks or months, involves multiple stakeholders, and requires tracking site visits, follow-ups, booking stages, and legal documentation — all in one place.

The Fortune Group's sales team was managing all of this across spreadsheets, WhatsApp, and a generic CRM that had been retrofitted with workarounds. It was slowing them down and causing lost leads.

My Role

Sole designer — complete ownership

I owned every stage of the design process: stakeholder workshops, user research, information architecture, user flows, wireframing, component library, high-fidelity UI, and developer handoff specs.

I also worked directly with the product manager and engineering leads to define the MVP scope and prioritise features for each sprint.

Act I · The Conflict

The Problem

Real estate sales teams were drowning in generic CRMs — tools built for tech startups, not property deals. Agents needed pipeline visibility, lead tracking, and workflow automation tailored to how property sales actually work. Existing tools had 80% features they'd never use and 20% they desperately needed — but in the wrong place, behind too many clicks, and with no mobile-first thinking.

Act II · Behind the Scenes

The Process

Started with stakeholder interviews and two weeks of sales team shadowing to map real workflows and pain points. Built user flows from scratch, ran three rounds of wireframe testing with sales agents, established a scalable component library, and iterated to high-fidelity prototypes. Collaborated directly with engineering to validate feasibility at every sprint boundary.

Act III · The Final Cut

The Solution

A clean, modular CRM with context-aware dashboards, drag-and-drop pipeline views, automated lead assignment logic, and a design system extensible to HRM modules. Built with a component-first approach so the engineering team could move fast. The result: a tool that sales teams actually want to open in the morning — not avoid until a manager asks for a report.

Learnings

What I'd do differently

With more time, I'd invest in a proper design token system from day one rather than retrofitting it mid-project. Colour, spacing, and type decisions made early became expensive to change at high-fidelity stage.

What worked well

The decisions that held up

Running structured walkthroughs with sales agents at the wireframe stage — not just at prototype stage — saved enormous rework time. Catching mental model mismatches early is always worth it.
